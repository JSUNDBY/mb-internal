The shape of the conversation

You're walking Melissa through two documents: the site report (what we found) and the pre-proposal (what we'd do). The goal of the call isn't to close, it's to land on the right scope. The price gets quoted after the call, in writing.

Make sure she knows up front: this is a new website. Not a tune-up, not a refresh, a complete new customer-facing site. The admin she logs into stays the same so her day-to-day product management doesn't change. Everything customers see is built from scratch. Be direct about this. If she only hears "rebuild" or "fix," she may picture a smaller scope than what we're actually doing.

Order of conversation:

  1. Set the frame: this is a new website. Brief and direct.
  2. Site report, short. The findings prove we actually looked at her current site.
  3. The new website (the core build), clarify it's required, non-negotiable.
  4. Growth services menu, this is where the conversation lives. Most of the time should be here.
  5. Platform additions, only if she leans in. Don't push.
  6. Scope conversation closes with: "Let me get this to Josh, you'll have a written number within 48 hours."

The one-line read

We're building you a brand-new website on modern infrastructure, Cloudflare for the front, Medusa for the back. Built for the next ten years of growth, not assembled from 2014 plugins. The WordPress admin you barely use because it's painful gets replaced by something pleasant.

If you only remember one sentence, that's it. Lead with "new website on modern infrastructure." Then make the admin point, the WP admin she avoids isn't a benefit we're protecting, it's a friction we're removing.

The opening move

Before walking through any documents, ask her this: "How often are you actually in the WordPress admin right now? Is it a place you spend time, or more something you avoid until you have to?"

Her answer tells you the whole conversation: - If she says "I avoid it", that's the opening to position Medusa as relief. The new admin is what she wishes WP was. - If she says "I'm in it constantly", soften the pitch; the new admin learning curve is bigger for her. Reassure her the new one is more pleasant, not just different.

Most likely she'll say "I avoid it because it's a nightmare." That's gold. Use it.

The architecture, in one paragraph

The new site lives on Cloudflare (the fastest delivery network on the internet, same one used by huge brands). The admin she logs into is Medusa, a modern, open-source ecommerce admin built specifically for stores. Not WordPress, not WooCommerce. A clean modern interface where adding a product takes two minutes from your phone if you want.

If she asks "do I have to leave WordPress?", yes, that's the point. But the migration is staged so nothing breaks. Her current site keeps running during the build. We move her products and data to staging. She tests it. We launch when she signs off. Her old site stays as a backup for 90 days. (Walk her through "How we move you off WordPress safely", page 3 of the pre-proposal.)

If she pushes back hard on leaving WordPress, there's a fallback. We can build the same new customer-facing site against her existing WooCommerce admin. She loses the cleaner admin but keeps her current login. Position this as the alternative, not the recommendation.

The admin mockup is your closer

There's a fourth document in the package: the admin mockup (/admin-mockup.html). It shows what her new admin will actually look like, dashboard, products list, add-product form, plus a side-by-side comparison of "today" vs "new" for three common tasks.

Show it during the call. Words don't sell the admin difference, the visual does. When she sees a clean two-column "Add Product" form next to the description of the WP/Elementor nightmare, the decision often makes itself.

How to talk about pricing (no numbers, on purpose)

Don't quote any number on this call. Not a ceiling, not a floor, not a range. The proposal intentionally doesn't anchor on a price because we want her to focus on what she wants before she sees a number. The minute a number is in the room, the conversation becomes about the number instead of the scope.

The frame to use: "The price depends on what you choose to include. Once we settle the scope, Josh quotes a single fixed number, no hourly billing, half on signature, half at launch. You'll have it in writing within 48 hours of this call."

If she presses for a ballpark: - "Honestly, I don't want to throw a number at you before we know what's in. Josh's number for a focused engagement is very different from a comprehensive one. Let's walk through what you want first, then I'll have him send you the real number tomorrow." - Repeat as needed. Don't budge. The scope conversation is the value.

If she really pushes: - "I can ask Josh to send you a written range right now if that helps, but my advice is to pick the scope first. Otherwise you'll anchor on the number instead of what you actually want the site to do." - If she insists, you can offer: "I'll get a directional range to you in writing within an hour.", then text Josh to write one quickly.

Why we work this way: Melissa is bright. She'll figure out very quickly that a fuller scope costs more. Letting her get there on her own, by picking what she wants, beats us announcing a number that triggers sticker shock before she's seen the value.

Never promise: specific traffic numbers, specific revenue lift, specific search rankings, or a timeline shorter than 6 weeks.

If she asks about... Say...

WebP / AVIF / image formats "Modern image formats that are smaller and faster. Your photos are still in older formats, which is part of why the site feels slow on phones."

Abandoned cart emails recover 10-15% of lost sales. These are well-documented effects. The compounding pieces, content, SEO, pay back over months, not weeks."

Reading Melissa during the call

If she's pacing fast / jumping around topics: - Slow down. Reset to the document. "Let me show you what we mean, page 2 of the report." - Don't try to address every direction she goes. Pick one thread and finish it.

If she's silent / thoughtful: - Give space. Don't fill silence with pitch. She's processing.

If she lights up on a specific growth service: - Stay there. That's the conversation. Don't move on to the next thing. Ask what about it resonated. - "That one seems like it's clicking, tell me what you're seeing."

If she goes negative: - Don't defend. Note it and move on. "Got it, we can leave that one out. What about the next one?"

What you don't need to answer

If Melissa asks anything technical you don't know, and she will, the answer is:

"Good question for Josh. Let me get the specific answer back to you tomorrow."

You don't need to know every technical detail. You need to know enough to keep the conversation moving and bring back precise questions for Josh to answer in writing. That's a feature, not a bug, clients prefer precise written answers to off-the-cuff verbal ones.

The strategic frame (subtext, not script)

The business needs to look like it's ready to scale. Investors care about growth, retention, infrastructure that can support 10x the volume. Everything in this document supports that read, but we never say it out loud. We talk about:

  • "Built to scale"
  • "Compounding channels"
  • "Recurring revenue" (subscriptions)
  • "New revenue channels" (wholesale, practitioner program)
  • "Stable, predictable" (email automation, subscriptions)

What we don't talk about: - Whether the current site is hurting investor confidence - Specific revenue projections - Competitor comparisons by name

After the call

Within an hour of hanging up, message Josh: 1. Which growth services Melissa said yes / maybe / no to 2. Any platform additions she leaned into 3. Anything she said she wanted that's not in this document 4. The timing pressure (when does she want this live?)

Josh writes the formal proposal from that. You see it before it goes to her. It goes out within 48 hours.
