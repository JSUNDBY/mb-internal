The shape of the conversation

You're walking Melissa through two documents: the site report (what we found) and the pre-proposal (what we'd do). The goal of the call isn't to close — it's to land on the right scope. The price gets quoted after the call, in writing.

Order of conversation:

  1. Site report first — short. The findings prove we actually looked.
  2. The foundation — clarify it's required, non-negotiable.
  3. Growth services menu — this is where the conversation lives. Most of the time should be here.
  4. Platform additions — only if she leans in. Don't push.
  5. Scope conversation closes with: "Let me get this to Josh, you'll have a number within 48 hours."

The one-line read

Your store engine is fine. Everything bolted on top of it is the problem. We strip the bolt-ons, add a real catalog, host it on Cloudflare so it loads in under a second — and you keep the same WooCommerce admin you log into today.

If you only remember one sentence, that's it.

The architecture, in one paragraph

The new site lives on Cloudflare (the fastest delivery network on the internet — same one used by huge brands). The admin she logs into is still WooCommerce — same place, same buttons, same workflow. We're rebuilding the half customers see, and leaving the half she uses alone. That's why this isn't scary: she doesn't have to relearn anything. Add a product in WooCommerce the way she always has → it shows up on the new fast site automatically.

If she asks "do I have to leave WordPress?" — no. That's an optional later upgrade called Modern Admin Migration (Medusa). Only if and when she wants a cleaner admin too. Not required, not pushed.

How to talk about pricing (no numbers, on purpose)

Don't quote any number on this call. Not a ceiling, not a floor, not a range. The proposal intentionally doesn't anchor on a price because we want her to focus on what she wants before she sees a number. The minute a number is in the room, the conversation becomes about the number instead of the scope.

The frame to use: "The price depends on what you choose to include. Once we settle the scope, Josh quotes a single fixed number — no hourly billing, half on signature, half at launch. You'll have it in writing within 48 hours of this call."

If she presses for a ballpark: - "Honestly, I don't want to throw a number at you before we know what's in. Josh's number for a focused engagement is very different from a comprehensive one. Let's walk through what you want first — then I'll have him send you the real number tomorrow." - Repeat as needed. Don't budge. The scope conversation is the value.

If she really pushes: - "I can ask Josh to send you a written range right now if that helps — but my advice is to pick the scope first. Otherwise you'll anchor on the number instead of what you actually want the site to do." - If she insists, you can offer: "I'll get a directional range to you in writing within an hour." — then text Josh to write one quickly.

Why we work this way: Melissa is bright. She'll figure out very quickly that a fuller scope costs more. Letting her get there on her own — by picking what she wants — beats us announcing a number that triggers sticker shock before she's seen the value.

Never promise: specific traffic numbers, specific revenue lift, specific search rankings, or a timeline shorter than 6 weeks.

Scope-by-scope selling points

Likely Melissa objections + how to handle

"We already have a website." - "You do, and it works. The problem isn't that it's broken — it's that it can't scale with the business. Right now every new product or change requires a developer. We're fixing the architecture so you own it."

"How fast will it pay back?" - "Hard to promise specific numbers, but here's what we know: catalog filters dramatically reduce bounce rate. Schema markup measurably increases search clicks. Abandoned cart emails recover 10-15% of lost sales. These are well-documented effects. The compounding pieces — content, SEO — pay back over months, not weeks."

"Why are we paying for a producer?" - "Because your time is better spent on the business than coordinating with a developer. I (Dawn) handle scheduling, feedback gathering, and decisions so Josh can stay heads-down on the build. You get one point of contact and faster execution."

"Can we start small and add later?" - "Yes — that's exactly how this is designed. The foundation is non-negotiable, but the growth services are pick-and-add. Most clients start with the foundation plus two growth services and layer the rest in later. The system is built to expand."

"Why not just fix what's wrong?" - "The technical issues we found aren't bugs — they're symptoms of an architecture problem. Patching them one by one costs more than the rebuild and leaves you exactly where you started: dependent on a developer for every change. We're fixing the root, not the leaves."

"I had bad experiences with developers before." - "Understood. That's exactly why we work the way we do: fixed price, fixed scope, one deposit, one delivery date. No hourly billing. Half on signature, half at launch. If something's not right at launch, we don't move on."

"I have a developer already." - "That's fine — they can stay involved. This isn't about replacing them, it's about giving you owner-managed control so you're not blocked on them for routine changes. They can handle the things that genuinely need a developer."

"Do I have to leave WordPress?" - "No. You keep logging into the same WooCommerce admin you use today. Same place, same buttons. We're only rebuilding what your customers see — that part moves to Cloudflare to make it fast. The half you use stays exactly where it is." - "If later on you decide you'd rather have a cleaner admin too, there's an optional upgrade path called Modern Admin Migration. But it's not required, and we're not pushing it."

"Why Cloudflare specifically?" - "It's the fastest delivery network on the internet — the same one big global brands use. For us, it means your customers see the site load almost instantly from anywhere in the world. And the plan we'd use is free at her scale, so it's not adding to her monthly costs."

"What if I want to add video?" - "That's one of the growth services. We can produce a brand story video, product education videos for top sellers, or both. Video on the homepage and product pages is one of the bigger conversion lifts available. Want me to leave that in the scope?"

Reading Melissa during the call

If she's pacing fast / jumping around topics: - Slow down. Reset to the document. "Let me show you what we mean — page 2 of the report." - Don't try to address every direction she goes. Pick one thread and finish it.

If she's silent / thoughtful: - Give space. Don't fill silence with pitch. She's processing.

If she lights up on a specific growth service: - Stay there. That's the conversation. Don't move on to the next thing. Ask what about it resonated. - "That one seems like it's clicking — tell me what you're seeing."

If she goes negative: - Don't defend. Note it and move on. "Got it, we can leave that one out. What about the next one?"

What you don't need to answer

If Melissa asks anything technical you don't know — and she will — the answer is:

"Good question for Josh. Let me get the specific answer back to you tomorrow."

You don't need to know every technical detail. You need to know enough to keep the conversation moving and bring back precise questions for Josh to answer in writing. That's a feature, not a bug — clients prefer precise written answers to off-the-cuff verbal ones.

The strategic frame (subtext, not script)

The business needs to look like it's ready to scale. Investors care about growth, retention, infrastructure that can support 10x the volume. Everything in this document supports that read — but we never say it out loud. We talk about:

  • "Built to scale"
  • "Compounding channels"
  • "Recurring revenue" (subscriptions)
  • "New revenue channels" (wholesale, practitioner program)
  • "Stable, predictable" (email automation, subscriptions)

What we don't talk about: - Whether the current site is hurting investor confidence - Specific revenue projections - Competitor comparisons by name

After the call

Within an hour of hanging up, message Josh: 1. Which growth services Melissa said yes / maybe / no to 2. Any platform additions she leaned into 3. Anything she said she wanted that's not in this document 4. The timing pressure (when does she want this live?)

Josh writes the formal proposal from that. You see it before it goes to her. It goes out within 48 hours.
